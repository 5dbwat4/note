**Problem 2.** A relation *r* has 400 blocks. Available memory is 20 blocks (i.e., M=20). External sort-merge is used to sort *r*. During the external sort-merge:

(1) What are the numbers of created runs for *r*? (15 points)

(2) What are the numbers of merge passes for *r*? (15 points)

<span style="color:red">(1) Answers: The numbers of created runs are 400/20=20.</span>

<span style="color:red">(2) Answers: As 20 > M-1, The numbers of merge passes are $\left\lceil \log_{M-1} 20 \right\rceil = 2$.</span>

**Problem 3.** Suppose that a B+-tree index on *building* is available on relation *department* and that no other index is available. What would be the best way to execute the following selections that involve negation based on the B+-tree index? (30 points)

(1)

$$
\sigma_{\neg(\text{build}<\text{"Watson"})}(\mathit{department})
$$

(2)

$$
\sigma_{\neg(\text{building}=\text{"Watson"})}(\mathit{department})
$$

(3)

$$
\sigma_{\neg(\text{build}<\text{"Watson"} \lor \text{budget}<50000)}(\mathit{department})
$$

<span style="color:red">(1) Use the index to locate the first tuple whose <em>building</em> field has value “Watson”. From this tuple, follow the pointer chains till the end, retrieving all the tuples.</span>

<span style="color:red">(2) For this query, the index serves no purpose. We can scan the file sequentially and select all tuples whose <em>building</em> field is anything other than “Watson”.</span>

<span style="color:red">(3) This query is equivalent to the query:</span>

<span style="color:red">$\sigma_{\text{building}\ge\text{"Watson"} \land \text{budget}\ge50000}(\mathit{department})$</span>

<span style="color:red">Using the <em>building</em> index, we can retrieve all tuples with <em>building</em> value greater than or equal to “Watson” by following the pointer chains from the first “Watson” tuple. We also apply the additional criteria of budget $\ge$ 50000 on every tuple.</span>
